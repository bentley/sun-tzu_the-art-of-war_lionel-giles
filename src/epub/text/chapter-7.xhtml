Chapter VII. Manœuvering347

1. Sun Tzu said: In war, the general receives his commands from the sovereign.348

2. Having collected an army and concentrated his forces, he must blend and harmonize the different elements thereof before pitching his camp.349

3. After that, comes tactical manœuvering, than which there is nothing more difficult.350

The difficulty of tactical manœuvering consists in turning the devious into the direct, and misfortune into gain.351

4. Thus, to take a long and circuitous route, after enticing the enemy out of the way, and though starting after him, to contrive to reach the goal before him, shows knowledge of the artifice of deviation.352

5. Manœuvering with an army is advantageous; with an undisciplined multitude, most dangerous.353

6. If you set a fully equipped army in march in order to snatch an advantage, the chances are that you will be too late.354 On the other hand, to detach a flying column for the purpose involves the sacrifice of its baggage and stores.355

7. Thus, if you order your men to roll up their buff-coats,356 and make forced marches without halting day or night, covering double the usual distance at a stretch,357

doing a hundred li in order to wrest an advantage, the leaders of all your three divisions will fall into the hands of the enemy.

8. The stronger men will be in front, the jaded ones will fall behind, and on this plan only one-tenth of your army will reach its destination.358

9. If you march fifty li in order to outmanœuver the enemy, you will lose the leader of your first division, and only half your force will reach the goal.360

10. If you march thirty li with the same object, two-thirds of your army will arrive.361

11. We may take it then that an army without its baggage-train is lost; without provisions it is lost; without bases of supply it is lost.362

12. We cannot enter into alliances until we are acquainted with the designs of our neighbors.363

13. We are not fit to lead an army on the march unless we are familiar with the face of the country⁠—its mountains and forests, its pitfalls364 and precipices,365 its marshes366 and swamps.367

14. We shall be unable to turn natural advantage to account unless we make use of local guides.368

15. In war, practice dissimulation, and you will succeed.369 Move only if there is a real advantage to be gained.371

16. Whether to concentrate or to divide your troops, must be decided by circumstances.

17. Let your rapidity be that of the wind,372

your compactness that of the forest.373

18. In raiding and plundering be like fire,374

in immovability like a mountain.375

19. Let your plans be dark and impenetrable as night, and when you move, fall like a thunderbolt.376

20. When you plunder a countryside, let the spoil be divided amongst your men;377

when you capture new territory, cut it up into allotments for the benefit of the soldiery.378

21. Ponder and deliberate379 before you make a move.380

22. He will conquer who has learnt the artifice of deviation.381

Such is the art of manœuvering.382

23. The Book of Army Management says:383

On the field of battle,384

the spoken word does not carry far enough: hence the institution of gongs and drums.385 Nor can ordinary objects be seen clearly enough: hence the institution of banners and flags.

24. Gongs and drums, banners and flags, are means whereby the ears and eyes of the host386 may be focused on one particular point.387

25. The host thus forming a single united body, is it impossible either for the brave to advance alone, or for the cowardly to retreat alone.388

This is the art of handling large masses of men.

26. In night-fighting, then, make much use of signal-fires and drums, and in fighting by day, of flags and banners, as a means of influencing the ears and eyes of your army.389

27. A whole army may be robbed of its spirit;390

a commander-in-chief may be robbed of his presence of mind.391

28. Now a soldier’s spirit is keenest in the morning;392

by noonday it has begun to flag; and in the evening, his mind is bent only on returning to camp.

29. A clever general, therefore,393 avoids an army when its spirit is keen, but attacks it when it is sluggish and inclined to return. This is the art of studying moods.394

30. Disciplined and calm, to await the appearance of disorder and hubbub amongst the enemy:⁠—this is the art of retaining self-possession.

31. To be near the goal while the enemy is still far from it, to wait at ease395 while the enemy is toiling and struggling, to be well-fed while the enemy is famished:⁠—this is the art of husbanding one’s strength.

32. To refrain from intercepting396 an enemy whose banners are in perfect order, to refrain from attacking an army drawn up in calm and confident array:397⁠—this is the art of studying circumstances.398

33. It is a military axiom not to advance uphill against the enemy, nor to oppose him when he comes downhill.399

34. Do not pursue an enemy who simulates flight; do not attack soldiers whose temper is keen.

35. Do not swallow bait offered by the enemy.400

Do not interfere with an army that is returning home.401

36. When you surround an army, leave an outlet free.402

Do not press a desperate foe too hard.403

37. Such is the art of warfare.404
