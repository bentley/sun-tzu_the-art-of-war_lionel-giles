Chapter XI. The Nine Situations569

1. Sun Tzǔ said: The art of war recognizes nine varieties of ground: (1) Dispersive ground; (2) facile ground; (3) contentious ground; (4) open ground; (5) ground of intersecting highways; (6) serious ground; (7) difficult ground; (8) hemmed-in ground; (9) desperate ground.

2. When a chieftain is fighting in his own territory, it is dispersive ground.570

3. When he has penetrated into hostile territory, but to no great distance, it is facile ground.571

4. Ground the possession of which imports great advantage to either side, is contentious ground.572

5. Ground on which each side has liberty of movement is open ground.573

6. Ground which forms the key to three contiguous states,574

so that he who occupies it first has most of the Empire at his command,575

is a ground of intersecting highways.576

7. When an army has penetrated into the heart of a hostile country, leaving a number of fortified cities in its rear,577 it is serious ground.578

8. Mountain forests,579

rugged steeps, marshes and fens⁠—all country that is hard to traverse: this is difficult ground.580

9. Ground which is reached through narrow gorges, and from which we can only retire by tortuous paths, so that a small number of the enemy would suffice to crush a large body of our men: this is hemmed in ground.

10. Ground on which we can only be saved from destruction by fighting without delay, is desperate ground.581

11. On dispersive ground, therefore, fight not. On facile ground, halt not. On contentious ground, attack not.582

12. On open ground, do not try to block the enemy’s way.583

On the ground of intersecting highways, join hands with your allies.584

13. On serious ground, gather in plunder.585

In difficult ground, keep steadily on the march.586

14. On hemmed-in ground, resort to stratagem.587

On desperate ground, fight.588

15. Those who were called skillful leaders of old589 knew how to drive a wedge between the enemy’s front and rear;590

to prevent co-operation between his large and small divisions; to hinder the good troops from rescuing the bad,591 the officers from rallying their men.592

16. When the enemy’s men were scattered, they prevented them from concentrating;593 even when their forces were united, they managed to keep them in disorder.594

17. When it was to their advantage, they made a forward move; when otherwise, they stopped still.595

18. If asked how to cope with a great host of the enemy in orderly array and on the point of marching to the attack,596 I should say: “Begin by seizing something which your opponent holds dear; then he will be amenable to your will.”597

19. Rapidity is the essence of war:598

take advantage of the enemy’s unreadiness, make your way by unexpected routes, and attack unguarded spots.

20. The following are the principles to be observed by an invading force: The further you penetrate into a country, the greater will be the solidarity of your troops, and thus the defenders will not prevail against you.

21. Make forays in fertile country in order to supply your army with food.599

22. Carefully study the well-being of your men,600

and do not overtax them. Concentrate your energy and hoard your strength.601

Keep your army continually on the move,602

and devise unfathomable plans.603

23. Throw your soldiers into positions whence there is no escape, and they will prefer death to flight.604 If they will face death, there is nothing they may not achieve.605

Officers and men alike will put forth their uttermost strength.606

24. Soldiers when in desperate straits lose the sense of fear. If there is no place of refuge, they will stand firm. If they are in hostile country, they will show a stubborn front.607 If there is no help for it, they will fight hard.

25. Thus, without waiting to be marshaled, the soldiers will be constantly on the qui vive;608 without waiting to be asked, they will do your will;609

without restrictions, they will be faithful;610 without giving orders, they can be trusted.611

26. Prohibit the taking of omens, and do away with superstitious doubts.612 Then, until death itself comes, no calamity need be feared.613

27. If our soldiers are not overburdened with money, it is not because they have a distaste for riches; if their lives are not unduly long, it is not because they are disinclined to longevity.614

28. On the day they are ordered out to battle, your soldiers may weep,615

those sitting up bedewing their garments, and those lying down letting the tears run down their cheeks.616

But let them once be brought to bay, and they will display the courage of a Chu or a Kuei.617

29. The skillful tactician may be likened to the shuai-jan. Now the shuai-jan is a snake that is found in the Chʽang mountains.618

Strike at its head, and you will be attacked by its tail; strike at its tail, and you will be attacked by its head; strike at its middle,619 and you will be attacked by head and tail both.

30. Asked if an army can be made to imitate the shuai-jan,620

I should answer, Yes. For the men of Wu and the men of Yüeh are enemies;621

yet if they are crossing a river in the same boat and are caught by a storm, they will come to each other’s assistance just as the left hand helps the right.622

31. Hence it is not enough to put one’s trust in the tethering of horses,623 and the burying of chariot wheels in the ground.624

32. The principle on which to manage an army is to set up one standard of courage which all must reach.625

33. How to make the best of both strong and weak⁠—that is a question involving the proper use of ground.626

34. Thus the skillful general conducts his army just as though he were leading a single man, willy-nilly, by the hand.628

35. It is the business of a general to be quiet and thus ensure secrecy; upright and just, and thus maintain order.629

36. He must be able to mystify his officers and men by false reports and appearances,630

and thus keep them in total ignorance.631

37. By altering his arrangements and changing his plans,633

he keeps the enemy without definite knowledge.634

By shifting his camp and taking circuitous routes, he prevents the enemy from anticipating his purpose.635

38. At the critical moment, the leader of an army acts like one who has climbed up a height and then kicks away the ladder behind him.636 He carries his men deep into hostile territory before he shows his hand.637

39. He burns his boats and breaks his cooking-pots;638 like a shepherd driving a flock of sheep, he drives his men this way and that, and nothing knows whither he is going.639

40. To muster his host and bring it into danger:⁠—this may be termed the business of the general.640

41. The different measures suited to the nine varieties of ground;641

the expediency of aggressive or defensive tactics;642 and the fundamental laws of human nature: these are things that must most certainly be studied.

42. When invading hostile territory, the general principle is, that penetrating deeply brings cohesion; penetrating but a short way means dispersion.644

43. When you leave your own country behind, and take your army across neighborhood territory,645 you find yourself on critical ground.646

When there are means of communication647 on all four sides, the ground is one of intersecting highways.648

44. When you penetrate deeply into a country, it is serious ground. When you penetrate but a little way, it is facile ground.

45. When you have the enemy’s strongholds on your rear,649 and narrow passes in front, it is hemmed-in ground. When there is no place of refuge at all, it is desperate ground.

46. Therefore, on dispersive ground, I would inspire my men with unity of purpose.650

On facile ground, I would see that there is close connection between all parts of my army.651

47. On contentious ground, I would hurry up my rear.652

48. On open ground, I would keep a vigilant eye on my defenses.653 On ground of intersecting highways, I would consolidate my alliances.654

49. On serious ground, I would try to ensure a continuous stream of supplies.655

On difficult ground, I would keep pushing on along the road.656

50. On hemmed-in ground, I would block any way of retreat.657

On desperate ground, I would proclaim to my soldiers the hopelessness of saving their lives.658

51. For it is the soldier’s disposition to offer an obstinate resistance when surrounded, to fight hard when he cannot help himself, and to obey promptly when he has fallen into danger.659

52. We cannot enter into alliance with neighboring princes until we are acquainted with their designs. We are not fit to lead an army on the march unless we are familiar with the face of the country⁠—its mountains and forests, its pitfalls and precipices, its marshes and swamps. We shall be unable to turn natural advantages to account unless we make use of local guides.660

53. To be ignored of any one of the following four or five principles661 does not befit a warlike prince.662

54. When a warlike prince attacks a powerful state, his generalship shows itself in preventing the concentration of the enemy’s forces. He overawes his opponents,663 and their allies are prevented from joining against him.664

55. Hence he does not strive665 to ally himself with all and sundry,666 nor does he foster the power of other states. He carries out his own secret designs,667 keeping his antagonists in awe.668

Thus he is able to capture their cities and overthrow their kingdoms.669

56. Bestow rewards without regard to rule,670

issue orders671

without regard to previous arrangements;672

and you will be able to handle a whole army673 as though you had to do with but a single man.674

57. Confront your soldiers with the deed itself; never let them know your design.675

When the outlook is bright, bring it before their eyes; but tell them nothing when the situation is gloomy.

58. Place your army in deadly peril, and it will survive; plunge it into desperate straits, and it will come off in safety.676

59. For it is precisely when a force has fallen into harm’s way that is capable of striking a blow for victory.677

60. Success in warfare is gained by carefully accommodating ourselves to the enemy’s purpose.678

61. By persistently hanging on the enemy’s flank,679

we shall succeed in the long run680

in killing the commander-in-chief.681

62. This is called ability to accomplish a thing by sheer cunning.682

63. On the day that you take up your command,683 block the frontier passes,684 destroy the official tallies,685

and stop the passage of all emissaries.686

64. Be stern in the council-chamber,687

so that you may control the situation.688

65. If the enemy leaves a door open, you must rush in.689

66. Forestall your opponent by seizing what he holds dear,690

and subtly contrive to time his arrival on the ground.691

67. Walk in the path defined by rule,692

and accommodate yourself to the enemy until you can fight a decisive battle.693

68. At first, then, exhibit the coyness of a maiden, until the enemy gives you an opening; afterwards emulate the rapidity of a running hare, and it will be too late for the enemy to oppose you.694
