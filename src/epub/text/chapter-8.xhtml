Chapter VIII. Variation of Tactics405

1. Sun Tzǔ said: In war, the general receives his commands from the sovereign, collects his army and concentrates his forces.406

2. When in difficult country, do not encamp.407 In country where high roads intersect, join hands with your allies.408 Do not linger in dangerously isolated positions.409

In hemmed-in situations, you must resort to stratagem.410 In desperate position, you must fight.411

3. There are roads which must not be followed,412

armies which must be not attacked,413

towns414 which must not be besieged,415

positions which must not be contested, commands of the sovereign which must not be obeyed.417

4. The general who thoroughly understands the advantages that accompany variation of tactics knows how to handle his troops.418

5. The general who does not understand these, may be well acquainted with the configuration of the country, yet he will not be able to turn his knowledge to practical account.419

6. So, the student of war who is unversed in the art of war of varying his plans, even though he be acquainted with the Five Advantages, will fail to make the best use of his men.420

7. Hence in the wise leader’s plans, considerations of advantage and of disadvantage will be blended together.421

8. If our expectation of advantage be tempered in this way, we may succeed in accomplishing the essential part of our schemes.422

9. If, on the other hand, in the midst of difficulties we are always ready to seize an advantage, we may extricate ourselves from misfortune.423

10. Reduce the hostile chiefs by inflicting damage on them;424

and make trouble for them,425

and keep them constantly engaged;426

hold out specious allurements, and make them rush to any given point.427

11. The art of war teaches us to rely not on the likelihood of the enemy’s not coming, but on our own readiness to receive him;428 not on the chance of his not attacking, but rather on the fact that we have made our position unassailable.429

12. There are five dangerous faults which may affect a general: (1) Recklessness, which leads to destruction;430

(2) cowardice, which leads to capture;431

(3) a hasty temper, which can be provoked by insults;432

(4) a delicacy of honor which is sensitive to shame;433

(5) over-solicitude for his men, which exposes him to worry and trouble.434

13. These are the five besetting sins of a general, ruinous to the conduct of war.

14. When an army is overthrown and its leader slain, the cause will surely be found among these five dangerous faults. Let them be a subject of meditation.
