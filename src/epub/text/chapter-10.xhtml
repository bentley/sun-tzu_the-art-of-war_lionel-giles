Chapter X. Terrain520

1. Sun Tzu said: We may distinguish six kinds of terrain, to wit: (1) Accessible ground;521

(2) entangling ground;522

(3) temporizing ground;523

(4) narrow passes; (5) precipitous heights;524 (6) positions at a great distance from the enemy.525

2. Ground which can be freely traversed by both sides is called accessible.526

3. With regard to ground of this nature,527 be before the enemy in occupying the raised and sunny spots,528 and carefully guard your line of supplies.529

Then you will be able to fight with advantage.532

4. Ground which can be abandoned but is hard to re-occupy is called entangling.533

5. From a position of this sort, if the enemy is unprepared, you may sally forth and defeat him. But if the enemy is prepared for your coming, and you fail to defeat him, then, return being impossible, disaster will ensue.534

6. When the position is such that neither side will gain by making the first move, it is called temporizing ground.535

7. In a position of this sort, even though the enemy should offer us an attractive bait,536

it will be advisable not to stir forth, but rather to retreat, thus enticing the enemy in his turn; then, when part of his army has come out, we may deliver our attack with advantage.537

8. With regard to narrow passes, if you can occupy them first,538 let them be strongly garrisoned and await the advent of the enemy.539

9. Should the army forestall you in occupying a pass, do not go after him if the pass is fully garrisoned, but only if it is weakly garrisoned.

10. With regard to precipitous heights, if you are beforehand with your adversary, you should occupy the raised and sunny spots, and there wait for him to come up.540

11. If the enemy has occupied them before you, do not follow him, but retreat and try to entice him away.541

12. If you are situated at a great distance from the enemy, and the strength of the two armies is equal,542 it is not easy to provoke a battle,543

and fighting will be to your disadvantage.

13. These six are the principles connected with Earth.544

The general who has attained a responsible post must be careful to study them.545

14. Now an army is exposed to six several calamities, not arising from natural causes,546 but from faults for which the general is responsible. These are: (1) Flight; (2) insubordination; (3) collapse; (4) ruin; (5) disorganization; (6) rout.547

15. Other conditions being equal, if one force is hurled against another ten times its size, the result will be the flight of the former.548

16. When the common soldiers are too strong and their officers too weak, the result is insubordination.549

When the officers are too strong and the common soldiers too weak, the result is collapse.550

17. When the higher officers551 are angry and insubordinate, and on meeting the enemy give battle on their own account from a feeling of resentment, before the commander-in-chief can tell whether or no he is in a position to fight, the result is ruin.552

18. When the general is weak and without authority; when his orders are not clear and distinct;553

when there are no fixes duties assigned to officers and men,555

and the ranks are formed in a slovenly haphazard manner, the result is utter disorganization.

19. When a general, unable to estimate the enemy’s strength, allows an inferior force to engage a larger one, or hurls a weak detachment against a powerful one, and neglects to place picked soldiers in the front rank, the result must be rout.556

20. These are six ways of courting defeat,557 which must be carefully noted by the general who has attained a responsible post.558

21. The natural formation of the country is the soldier’s best ally;559

but a power of estimating the adversary,560 of controlling the forces of victory,561 and of shrewdly calculating difficulties, dangers and distances,562 constitutes the test of a great general.563

22. He who knows these things, and in fighting puts his knowledge into practice, will win his battles. He who knows them not, nor practices them, will surely be defeated.

23. If fighting is sure to result in victory, then you must fight, even though the ruler forbid it; if fighting will not result in victory, then you must not fight even at the ruler’s bidding.564

24. The general who advances without coveting fame and retreats without fearing disgrace,566

whose only thought is to protect his country and do good service for his sovereign,567 is the jewel of the kingdom.568

25. Regard your soldiers as your children, and they will follow you into the deepest valleys; look upon them as your own beloved sons, and they will stand by you even unto death.569

26. If, however, you are indulgent, but unable to make your authority felt; kind-hearted, but unable to enforce your commands; and incapable, moreover, of quelling disorder:570 then your soldiers must be likened to spoilt children; they are useless for any practical purpose.571

27. If we know that our own men are in a condition to attack, but are unaware that the enemy is not open to attack, we have gone only halfway towards victory.572

28. If we know that the enemy is open to attack, but are unaware that our own men are not in a condition to attack, we have gone only halfway towards victory.573

29. If we know that the enemy is open to attack, and also know that our men are in a condition to attack, but are unaware that the nature of the ground makes fighting impracticable, we have still gone only halfway towards victory.574

30. Hence the experienced soldier, once in motion, is never bewildered; once he has broken camp, he is never at a loss.575

31. Hence the saying: If you know the enemy and know yourself, your victory will not stand in doubt;576 if you know Heaven and know Earth,577 you may make your victory complete.578
