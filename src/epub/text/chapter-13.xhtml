Chapter XIII. The Use of Spies739

1. Sun Tzu said: Raising a host of a hundred thousand men and marching them great distances entails heavy loss on the people and a drain on the resources of the State. The daily expenditure will amount to a thousand ounces of silver.740

There will be commotion at home and abroad, and men will drop down exhausted on the highways.741

As many as seven hundred thousand families will be impeded in their labor.742

2. Hostile armies may face each other for years, striving for the victory which is decided in a single day. This being so, to remain in ignorance of the enemy’s condition simply because one grudges the outlay of a hundred ounces of silver in honors and emoluments,743

is the height of inhumanity.744

3. One who acts thus is no leader of men, no present help to his sovereign,745 no master of victory.746

4. Thus, what enables the wise sovereign and the good general to strike and conquer, and achieve things beyond the reach of ordinary men, is foreknowledge.747

5. Now this foreknowledge cannot be elicited from spirits;748 it cannot be obtained inductively from experience,749

nor by any deductive calculation.750

6. Knowledge of the enemy’s dispositions can only be obtained from other men.751

7. Hence the use of spies, of whom there are five classes: (1) Local spies; (2) inward spies; (3) converted spies; (4) doomed spies; (5) surviving spies.

8. When these five kinds of spy are all at work, none can discover the secret system.752 This is called753 “divine manipulation of the threads.”754 It is the sovereign’s most precious faculty.755

9. Having local spies757 means employing the services of the inhabitants of a district.758

10. Having inward spies, making use of officials of the enemy.759

11. Having converted spies, getting hold of the enemy’s spies and using them for our own purposes.760

12. Having doomed spies, doing certain things openly for purposes of deception, and allowing our spies to know of them and report them to the enemy.761

13. Surviving spies, finally, are those who bring back news from the enemy’s camp.763

14. Hence it is that which none in the whole army are more intimate relations to be maintained than with spies.765

None should be more liberally rewarded.766 In no other business should greater secrecy be preserved.767

15. Spies cannot be usefully employed769 without a certain intuitive sagacity.770

16. They cannot be properly managed without benevolence and straightforwardness.771

17. Without subtle ingenuity of mind, one cannot make certain of the truth of their reports.772

18. Be subtle! be subtle!773 and use your spies for every kind of business.

19. If a secret piece of news is divulged by a spy before the time is ripe, he must be put to death together with the man to whom the secret was told.774

20. Whether the object be to crush an army, to storm a city, or to assassinate an individual, it is always necessary to begin by finding out the names of the attendants,775 the aides-de- camp,776

and door-keepers and sentries777 of the general in command.778 Our spies must be commissioned to ascertain these.779

21. The enemy’s spies who have come to spy on us must be sought out,780 tempted with bribes, led away and comfortably housed.781 Thus they will become converted spies and available for our service.

22. It is through the information brought by the converted spy that we are able to acquire and employ local and inward spies.782

23. It is owing to his information, again, that we can cause the doomed spy to carry false tidings to the enemy.783

24. Lastly, it is by his information that the surviving spy can be used on appointed occasions.784

25. The end and aim of spying in all its five varieties is knowledge of the enemy;785 and this knowledge can only be derived, in the first instance, from the converted spy.786

Hence it is essential that the converted spy be treated with the utmost liberality.

26. Of old, the rise of the Yin dynasty787

was due to I Chih788

who had served under the Hsia. Likewise, the rise of the Chou dynasty was due to Lu Ya789

who had served under the Yin.790

27. Hence it is only the enlightened ruler and the wise general who will use the highest intelligence of the army for purposes of spying791 and thereby they achieve great results.792

Spies are a most important element in war, because on them depends an army’s ability to move.793
